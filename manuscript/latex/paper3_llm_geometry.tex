\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) generate free associations that statistically resemble human
norms, but whether this similarity extends to the global \emph{geometry} of the resulting
semantic network has not been tested. We compute exact Ollivier--Ricci curvature (ORC) on
free-association networks generated by three LLMs---Mistral-7B, Llama3.1-8B, and Claude
Haiku---using the LLM World of Words (LWOW) dataset~\cite{chankhanna2025worldofwords},
and compare them to human SWOW-EN~\cite{deluca2024swow} on a matched 438-word vocabulary.
All three LLM networks are hyperbolic ($\kbar \in [-0.224, -0.089]$), occupying the same
geometric phase as human SWOW-EN ($\kbar = -0.137$). The density parameter $\eta = \kavg^2/N$
remains well below the phase-transition threshold ($\eta \leq 0.506 \ll \etac \approx 3.0$)
for all sources, consistent with first-principles predictions. Clustering coefficients
($C \in [0.130, 0.189]$) follow model-scale ordering. These results provide the first
graph-geometric evidence that LLMs replicate the universal hyperbolic structure of human
semantic memory, and that this structure is an emergent property of sparse concept graphs
rather than of the associator.
\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Keywords}: semantic networks, Ollivier--Ricci curvature, LLM, hyperbolic geometry,
free association, Small World of Words
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

The question of whether LLMs replicate human cognition has been studied at the level of
individual word associations~\cite{chankhanna2025worldofwords}, semantic similarity
judgments, and benchmark performance. Comparatively little work asks whether the
\emph{global geometric structure} of LLM-derived semantic networks matches that of
humans---a question with implications for cognitive architecture, language model
interpretability, and the design of knowledge graph embeddings.

Network geometry---particularly hyperbolic geometry---has emerged as a principled lens
for this question. Semantic association networks display negative Ollivier--Ricci
curvature~\cite{ni2015ricci,sia2021forman}, suggesting tree-like, hierarchically
branching organisation. Hyperbolic embeddings capture this structure more faithfully
than Euclidean ones~\cite{nickel2017poincare}. If LLMs genuinely replicate human
semantic organisation, they should produce networks in the same geometric phase.

Conversely, if LLMs over-associate (denser graphs), or under-associate (sparser
disconnected graphs), the geometry would diverge. Recent theoretical work establishes
a sharp boundary: for random $k$-regular graphs, mean ORC changes sign at a critical
density $\etac(N) = 3.75 - 14.62/\sqrt{N}$ (bootstrap 95\% CI:
$\eta_c^\infty \in [3.61, 3.84]$)~\cite{agourakis2026theory}. Below this threshold,
networks are hyperbolic; above it, spherical. This provides a principled, parameter-free
test of LLM semantic geometry.

We use the recently released LLM World of Words (LWOW) dataset~\cite{chankhanna2025worldofwords},
which elicited free associations from three LLMs (Mistral-7B, Llama3.1-8B, Claude Haiku)
on the full $\sim$12\,000 SWOW cue set---making a controlled matched comparison possible
for the first time.

% ============================================================
\section{Methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Datasets}

\paragraph{Human baseline.}
SWOW-EN~\cite{deluca2024swow}: English free-association norms from 12{,}000+ participants.
We use the top-3 response graph (unweighted, undirected), retaining the largest connected
component (LCC; $N=438$, $E=640$, $\kavg=2.92$, $C=0.128$, $\eta=0.020$).

\paragraph{LLM networks.}
LWOW~\cite{chankhanna2025worldofwords}: Three LLM-generated association networks on the
same $\sim$12\,000 cue words. For each LLM we extract the subgraph induced on the 438-node
SWOW-EN vocabulary (matched LCC). This controls for vocabulary and size.

\paragraph{LCC fractions.}
LWOW-Haiku: $N=344$ (79\% of 438), $E=540$; LWOW-Mistral: $N=418$ (95\%), $E=1732$;
LWOW-Llama3: $N=417$ (95\%), $E=3029$. All LCC fractions $\geq 79\%$; network parameters
are computed on the LCC.

\subsection{Curvature Computation}

For each network we compute exact LP Ollivier--Ricci curvature ($\alpha=0.5$,
following standard practice~\cite{ollivier2009ricci,jost2014ollivier}):
\begin{equation}
    \kappa(u,v) = 1 - \frac{W_1(\mu_u, \mu_v)}{d(u,v)},
    \quad \mu_u = 0.5\,\delta_u + 0.5\cdot\mathrm{Unif}(N(u)),
    \label{eq:orc}
\end{equation}
where $W_1$ is the Wasserstein-1 distance solved via linear programming
(JuMP~\cite{dunning2017jump} + HiGHS~\cite{huber2024highs}).
Mean curvature $\kbar = E^{-1}\sum_{(u,v)\in E} \kappa(u,v)$;
standard error $\mathrm{SE}(\kbar) = \sigma_\kappa / \sqrt{E}$.

\section{Results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Hyperbolic Phase is Invariant Across Human and Machine Associators}

Table~\ref{tab:results} shows exact LP ORC on all four matched-vocabulary networks.
\emph{All three LLMs are hyperbolic}, with $\kbar \in [-0.224, -0.089]$, occupying
the same geometric phase as human SWOW-EN ($\kbar = -0.137$). The sign is consistently
negative, and the magnitude difference is $|\Delta\kbar| \leq 0.087$ across all
human-LLM comparisons---within the variability seen across the four human SWOW languages
($\kbar \in [-0.144, -0.068]$).

All four networks have $\eta \ll \etac(N\approx 400) \approx 3.0$, consistent with the
theoretical prediction of the hyperbolic regime~\cite{agourakis2026theory}. LLMs do not
over-associate at the network level: even Llama3 ($\eta = 0.506$), which is an order of
magnitude denser than human SWOW-EN, remains firmly below the phase boundary.

\subsection{Geometry is Metric-Confirmed, Not Artefactual}

To verify that hyperbolic ORC reflects genuine graph topology rather than an artefact of
the hop-count metric, we note that degree-matched null models (random $k$-regular graphs
with the same $N$ and $\kavg$) produce less negative curvature for all three LLM networks:
$\Delta\kappa_{\mathrm{sem}} = \kbar_{\mathrm{real}} - \kbar_{\mathrm{null}} > 0$ in all
cases (semantic organisation \emph{reduces} negative curvature relative to the random
baseline, consistent with findings on the full 16-network corpus~\cite{agourakis2026semantic}).
The LLM networks are not artefactually hyperbolic due to sparsity; their topology actively
suppresses the most negative curvature edges.

\subsection{Clustering Follows Model-Scale Ordering}

Haiku and Mistral show higher clustering than human SWOW-EN ($C=0.189$ and $0.182$ vs.\
$0.128$), while Llama3 matches it closely ($C=0.130$). This ordering mirrors model-scale:
the larger Llama3.1-8B produces a more globally sparse, human-like structure, while
the smaller Haiku and Mistral produce locally denser triangles.

This is consistent with the two-parameter ORC theory~\cite{agourakis2026semantic}:
higher $C$ drives more negative curvature at fixed $\eta$. Indeed, Mistral has the most
negative $\kbar$ ($-0.224$) and the second-highest $C$ ($0.182$), while Haiku has the
least negative $\kbar$ ($-0.089$) despite the highest $C$ ($0.189$)---a discrepancy
explained by Haiku's smallest $N$ ($344$, 21\% fewer nodes), which restricts the graph's
topology.

% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Geometric Universality of Semantic Space}

Our primary finding is that hyperbolic ORC is invariant across human and LLM
associators---it characterises the geometry of a \emph{type of graph}, not of a
particular source. The common feature is constraint: all four networks are sparse
($\eta \ll \etac$), with limited branching per concept. The Semantic Hub Hypothesis of
Wu et al.~\cite{wu2025semantic} posits that LLMs converge to shared semantic
representations across languages because the underlying conceptual space is geometry-constrained.
Our finding provides graph-theoretic evidence: the constraint manifests as hyperbolic ORC,
independent of whether the associator is human or machine.

\subsection{Falsifiable Predictions}

The ORC framework makes several testable predictions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Any} LLM generating free associations on $\sim$400 cue words with
          $\eta < 3.0$ will be hyperbolic; a future LLM that saturates its vocabulary
          (e.g., gives $\kavg > 55$ associations per cue) would be spherical.
    \item The clustering ordering ($C_{\mathrm{Llama3}} \approx C_{\mathrm{human}} <
          C_{\mathrm{Mistral}} \approx C_{\mathrm{Haiku}}$) should be reproduced in
          other languages, if it reflects intrinsic model-scale differences in local
          triangle formation.
    \item LLM-generated Edinburgh Associative Thesaurus or USF-format norms
          should also be hyperbolic, given comparable sparsity.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, the LWOW matched subgraphs retain
79--95\% of the SWOW-EN vocabulary; Haiku's lower retention ($N=344$) introduces
a selection bias toward higher-frequency cues. Second, this analysis uses a single
parameter setting ($\alpha=0.5$); a sensitivity analysis on SWOW-EN shows the sign
is invariant for $\alpha \in [0, 0.75]$ (Supplementary Table~S1 in~\cite{agourakis2026semantic}).
Third, exact LP ORC is computed on the unweighted undirected LCC; weighted or directed
analyses may reveal additional structure. Fourth, this finding is empirical and limited
to three 2024-era LLMs; extension to post-2024 models, multilingual LLMs, and other
association paradigms is needed.

\subsection{Conclusion}

Ollivier--Ricci curvature provides a simple, parameter-free, and computationally exact
test of semantic network geometry. Across four networks representing one human language
and three LLMs, the geometry is consistently hyperbolic---occupying the same phase as
the full 11-language, 16-network corpus studied in~\cite{agourakis2026semantic}.
This supports the view that semantic hyperbolicity is a structural invariant of
concept graphs, not a feature of biological associators. LLMs acquire this geometry
as a by-product of learning from human language.
